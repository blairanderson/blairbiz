## Current Situation

Looped has two distinct service buckets:

1. **Creative solutions** — unusual materials (dead trout, expired butter, raw chicken, plastic liners) where the value add is figuring out the best non-obvious end use and finding the right buyer. This requires active R&D. "The two questions we always ask are: what's the non-obvious use for it, and what do we have to do to get it to the highest-value point?"

2. **Speed / emergency response** — time-sensitive account management: scheduling trucks, confirming pickups, managing day-to-day requests from recurring clients, and reacting to unplanned shutdowns. "You need to quote in an hour and be on site in six hours." Some jobs are sub-24 hours.

Revenue model: they mediate between clients (companies with waste) and customers (receiving/processing facilities). Money can flow both ways — they sometimes pay clients for valuable material and charge processors, charge clients for removal and pay processors, or both. Freight is always arranged by Looped; proximity is a margin variable.

Freight is currently handled by one independent broker, two larger broker portals, and a full-time person in Macedonia who researches freight 8 hours/day. "When it's midnight there, she's off the clock." They want to expand beyond existing brokers to hit load boards too.

Back office: C-Trade (~$1,500–1,600/month) manages all load records. It integrates with QuickBooks Online but the integration is partial — staff still manually post from C-Trade to QuickBooks and verify invoice amounts. Invoices come into a shared AP email and "just sit there. There's no automation involved." Andrew handles the day-to-day accounting on top of COO duties.

They're growing faster than expected. The operational back office hasn't caught up.

## Pain Points Discussed

- **Research is eating 20 hours per lead.** After a lead hits Trello, the real work is finding buyers and pricing. "The pain point is the 20 hours that go into finding that phone call." Sales reps are better at cold calling than research, so Tommy, Andrew, and Cort often have to step in.

- **Freight quoting is manual and coverage-limited.** Comparing carriers, checking portals, and calling brokers is time-intensive. The Macedonia researcher covers it during her window but "when it's midnight there, she's off the clock." They want real-time coverage across more sources including load boards.

- **AP inbox is a black hole.** Invoices hit the AP email and stop. No extraction, no routing, no reconciliation. Andrew described it as "that's why Andrew doesn't sleep anymore" and "we're living in the Stone Age." Manual posting from C-Trade to QuickBooks adds another layer.

- **Account management is phone-heavy and error-prone.** Scheduling trucks, confirming dock instructions, tracking weights, generating documents — all done by humans on phones, often in real time. They just hired two more people for this. "Scheduling trucks, confirming requests from day-to-day clients, moving the day-to-day flow along, extracting data from invoices, getting it ready to input into C-Trade, generating documents" — all named explicitly.

- **Sales calls leave detail on the floor.** "Your salespeople are probably leaving a lot of useful detail out of the Trello cards that AI could capture." Trello cards from the sales team are less detailed than the ones Cort creates himself. They hated traditional meeting-bot UX but were receptive to Granola's approach (no bot in the meeting, just background audio capture).

## Opportunities Identified

1. **AP email → invoice extraction → QuickBooks reconciliation (highest value, Andrew's #1).** Intake pipeline reads AP email attachments, extracts invoice data, validates against C-Trade records, flags mismatches, and prepares QuickBooks postings. Low interference risk — this is entirely internal, nothing customer-facing.

2. **Account management ops copilot.** Turns incoming requests into structured tasks, drafts confirmations, tracks status, and generates standard documents around each load. Reduces the phone-to-spreadsheet loop for the CS team. Directly tied to their headcount efficiency goal.

3. **Freight rate automation.** Always-on quoting that pulls lane + material from Trello/C-Trade, hits broker portals and load boards, compares options, and returns the best move — with 24/7 coverage beyond the Macedonia work window.

4. **Research agent for outlet discovery.** Takes a material card, identifies likely end uses, surfaces nearby buyers/processors, finds direct contacts and phone numbers, returns a prioritized "who to call" list with indicative pricing. Their own framing: "finds our best options, gives us a pricing guide, and tees up exactly who we need to call."

5. **Sales call transcription → Trello enrichment.** Granola-style (no bot, background audio capture) generates call summaries, extracts specs and open questions, and updates the relevant Trello card. Coaching prompts like "did you ask about cardboard?" could follow.

6. **Lead-list building for cold outreach.** Not email blasting — just building targeted contact lists. "Give me 20 cattle farmers within 100 miles and their phone numbers." The research agent could feed this naturally.
