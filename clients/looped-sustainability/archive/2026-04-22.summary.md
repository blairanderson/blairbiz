## Current Situation

Looped matches clients (manufacturers, distributors, retailers) with receiving facilities for material diversion — food waste, cardboard, plastics, meat byproducts, anything that would otherwise go to landfill. Revenue model varies: sometimes they sell material, sometimes they charge clients, sometimes both on the same job. The constant is logistics: they always arrange the truck.

They're growing. They hired two new customer service reps the day before this call. Primary systems are CIETrade (~$1,500–1,600/month and scaling up with headcount) for all operational record-keeping, Trello for sales pipeline and per-account boards, and ChatGPT ad hoc for R&D research. CIETrade has no public API — their data is in a walled garden.

Jobs arrive as emails ("24 pallets of strawberries") and urgent phone calls. Many are same-day or sub-24-hour turnaround. Unusual loads (3 million lbs of dead trout, raw chicken) require active research to find the right buyer or end use.

## Pain Points Discussed

- **Emergency quoting bottleneck.** They often need a full carrier + outlet quote within one hour. Right now that's a manual scramble — identify the outlet, get pricing, get carrier pricing, combine into a quote. Limits how fast they can scale.
- **Logistics coordination is phone-heavy.** Every pickup involves confirming dock instructions, security codes, facility readiness, and carrier availability. Unplanned facility shutdowns happen. All of it goes through humans, in real time.
- **R&D on unusual materials is slow and incomplete.** Figuring out the highest-value end use for something like 3M lbs/year of dead trout (Centralia, WA) requires manual research. ChatGPT helps but inconsistently, and they don't know what they're missing.
- **Finding buyers for niche loads is relationship-dependent.** The Alabama bait-solution guy for the trout — that's the kind of connection they need but can't systematically find. Cold email to unknown contacts won't work in this industry; they need warm, targeted outreach.
- **CIETrade has no API.** Can't automate data in or out. Staff manually enters weights, costs, and job details after the fact. It's functional but a ceiling on automation.
- **Intake is unstructured.** No standard format for incoming jobs. Each email or call requires a human to parse it and create the record.

## Opportunities Identified

1. **Trello research bot (highest priority, lowest risk).** An AI agent that watches a dedicated Trello board — whenever a new card appears (a material or job), it auto-researches best end uses, potential buyers, and target industries, then comments directly on the card. No client or vendor exposure. Trello's open API makes this straightforward. Cort lit up on this one.

2. **Emergency quote engine.** A semi-automated system that, when an emergency job comes in, simultaneously blasts rate requests to all relevant carriers and outlets based on material type and geography, then assembles the options. Combines custom app logic (structured database + math) with AI for the messier matching pieces.

3. **BOL and document parsing.** AI reads incoming PDFs (bills of lading, weight tickets), extracts key fields (weights, costs, job details), and exports to Excel or pushes to a third-party reporting system. Confirmed this is fully feasible.

4. **Logistics coordination automation.** Automated check-in messages and calls before pickups — confirm dock availability, flag unplanned shutdowns, alert the team if something changes. Phone call AI is 12+ months out from being plug-and-play reliable, but email/text workflows are available now.

5. **Custom logistics management app (longer-term).** If they decide to build around CIETrade rather than inside it, a lightweight custom app could handle order tracking, weight entry, and coordination — with CIETrade staff reduced to data entry only.

## What They're Using Now

- **CIETrade** — primary ops and reporting system. No API. Client portal included. ~$1,500–1,600/month.
- **Trello** — sales pipeline + per-account boards. Full open API.
- **ChatGPT** — ad hoc R&D. No systematic use.
- **Phone + email** — primary intake and coordination channels.
- **QuickBooks** — partial integration via CIETrade.

## Concerns / Objections

- **Data exposure via APIs.** Cort asked directly: if they open up Trello's API, could competitors read their deal pricing? Blair addressed this clearly — the Trello research bot has no exposure risk (data stays in their Trello), and any email-based agent can be locked to specific senders only.
- **AI reliability.** Blair was honest: AI still gets things wrong, especially in agent workflows. Framed it as "sometimes feels like babysitting." This landed well — they appreciated the candor.
- **Sending automated emails.** Both sides agreed you don't want AI auto-sending cold emails to unknown contacts. Research and identification yes; outreach needs to be warm and human-initiated.

## What We Agreed On

- The Trello research bot is the right first experiment: lowest risk, high value, doesn't touch any client or vendor.
- Before Blair builds anything custom, Cort and Andrew should try **getVictor.com** — a commercial AI agent product that plugs into Trello and other tools. ~$50/month. If it does what they need, great. If it falls short, that's the signal to bring Blair in for custom work.
- Blair offered a retainer model ("be your AI guy, some hours a week") rather than one-off projects — fits what they actually need as they figure out what to build.
